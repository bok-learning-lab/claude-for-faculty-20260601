Zuppa Pavese

Pavia Soup — serves 6

About this recipe

Zuppa Pavese comes from Pavia, a small city in Lombardy in northern Italy, and it has one of the best origin legends in Italian cooking. The story — almost certainly embellished — goes that in February 1525, after the Battle of Pavia where King Francis I of France was captured by the army of Charles V, the defeated king was brought to a farmhouse exhausted and hungry. The peasant woman had nothing to feed a king except day-old bread, eggs, broth, and a piece of cheese, so she fried the bread in butter, cracked an egg over it, and poured hot broth on top. Francis is supposed to have called it "the soup of a king," and the dish kept the name of the battle ever since.

The cleverness of the recipe is in the construction. The toasted bread floats on top, the egg poaches in the heat of the broth as it's poured over (no separate poaching step), and the Parmesan melts into a savory crust. It's a peasant zuppa in the strict sense — Italian cooks distinguish zuppa (broth poured over bread) from minestra (broth with things cooked in it) — and you can see in this handwritten version the survival of an almost 500-year-old technique unchanged: bread, egg, broth, cheese, with the heat doing all the cooking at the table.

Ingredients

  • 3 tbsp butter
  • 6 slices firm, coarse-textured bread
  • 6 cups meat or chicken broth
  • Salt
  • 6 eggs
  • Freshly ground pepper
  • 6 tbsp freshly grated Parmesan cheese
  • Parsley and nutmeg for garnish

Directions

  1. Melt butter in a saucepan and fry the bread slices on both sides until golden brown. Divide among 6 soup bowls. Place bowls in an oven that has been pre-heated to 350°F and turned off.
  2. Meanwhile, bring broth to a boil, adding salt if necessary.
  3. Break an egg onto each slice of bread, pour the boiling broth over it, and add pepper to taste.
  4. Sprinkle with Parmesan cheese. Garnish with parsley and nutmeg.

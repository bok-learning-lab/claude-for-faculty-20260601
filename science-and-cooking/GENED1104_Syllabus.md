This course will discuss concepts from the physical sciences that underpin both everyday cooking and haute cuisine. The content, including the delicious and edible (!) labs, have been developed in collaboration with the El Bulli Foundation in Spain. Each week a world-class chef will visit and present their remarkable culinary designs. For 2025, the chefs include José Andrés (minibar, ThinkFoodGroup, World Central Kitchen), Joanne Chang (Flour Bakery and Café, Myers and Chang, Boston), Lars Williams (Empirical Spirits, Copenhagen, NYC), Bryan Furman (BBQ Master, Atlanta), Claire Saffitz (author of Dessert person), Smitha Haneef and Martin Breslin (Harvard University Dining Services), Nok Suntaranon (Kalaya, Philadelphia), Mauro Colagreco (Mirazur, France). There will also be demos and lectures by other leaders in the field, including flavor scientist Dave Arnold (author of Liquid Intelligence) and Harold McGee (author of On Food and Cooking, Keys to Good Cooking).

At the end of the course, students will be able to explain how a range of cooking techniques and recipes work, in terms of the physical and chemical transformations of the food.

## Expectations

Attendance at two weekly lectures and one 2-hour section are required and count toward the participation grade. Attendance at one Chef Chat during the course of the semester is also required. You are expected to submit weekly problem sets and lab worksheets, submit seven chef lecture reflections, take two midterm exams, and write and present a final research project. The class will have no final exam.

## Lectures

**Lecture attendance is required.** Lectures will be held in Science Center Hall D on Tuesdays and Thursdays from 12 to 1:15 pm. Tuesday lectures begin with a 20-minute introduction about the relevant scientific topic, followed by a presentation by a visiting chef who discusses culinary applications of this topic. Thursday lectures are led by the course instructors and go deeper into the science. A portion of the material covered on Thursdays will be especially designed to prepare you for the week's homework. Some, but not all, lectures will be video-recorded and posted on the Panopto tab on Canvas. **Note:** out of respect for the chefs, no laptops can be used during their presentations.

## Lab Sections

**Lab attendance is required.** Sections and labs are combined into a single 2-hour block, at which attendance is required. In the past, this has been the highlight of the course for most students—you will make measurements on recipes that you can eat! The first half hour of each section will be devoted to a review of scientific concepts. Following this, you will prepare a recipe in a team of 2–3 students, and make measurements along the way, to illustrate the concepts discussed in lecture. At the end of each section you are expected to turn in a brief **lab worksheet**. Labs take place in our food-safe lab/kitchen in Maxwell Dworkin G116.

**Note:** The course involves the preparation and (optional) consumption of food. If you have specific food allergies or needs, please be in touch with the course staff to discuss arrangements.

You are required to attend the lab to which you have been assigned. You will not be permitted to attend another lab without permission from the head-TF. You will receive a zero for the lab if you miss your assigned lab section without making prior arrangements to attend a different lab time. Non-emergency requests (job interviews or similar) to switch section should be submitted at least 48 hours in advance. Unfortunately, not all swap requests can be honored because of space limitations in the lab. All section switching must be confirmed prior to section by the head-TF.

In event of illness, you should not come to lab! Please instead see UHS (University Health Services), and you may either use your dropped lab (lowest worksheet score is dropped—see next section) to be excused from the section or attend another lab section later in the week (as above, email the head-TF for arrangements). As you will still be expected to understand material for all labs, you should still review the lab worksheet for any missed labs. Sample data for this purpose can be provided upon request. For extenuating circumstances, email your TF for assistance. Unfortunately, make-up labs are not available outside regularly scheduled lab times or past the week when a specific lab is held.

**Tardiness:** It is crucial that you arrive on time to your assigned lab section. Lab doors will open 5 minutes prior to the start of lab. Lab doors will be closed promptly 10 minutes after the start of lab. For example, if your lab section starts at 12pm, doors will open at 11:55am and they will close at 12:10 pm. Students will not be admitted to lab if they arrive after the lab doors have been closed. Arriving late to lab will be considered an unexcused absence, and you will receive a grade of 'zero' for that lab.

## Lab Worksheet

The lab worksheet is a brief but integral part of lab and contains questions about the experiments, your results, and data analysis. The worksheet is due at the end of each lab section. **You are allowed to drop your one lowest worksheet score.** This drop can be used if you need to miss a section.

## Homework / Problem Set

**Weekly homework assignments are due to be uploaded to Canvas on Tuesdays at noon.** Late homeworks (turned in within 24 hours after the deadline) will be eligible for half-credit (so a maximum score for a late 100 point assignment is 50 points); homeworks turned in more than 24 hours after the deadline are given no credit. However, if you are late and have an emergency, AND let your TF know ahead of time, we may be able to give you more credit. No credit can be given after the answer key has been posted. **Your lowest homework score will be dropped.**

If you have a documented medical or family emergency that prevents you from completing the assignment, you will be excused from submitting that week's problem set. To be excused from submitting a problem set, your emergency must be documented by a note from your doctor, or from University Health Services, or by a resident dean, or other university official. If no documentation is provided, the missed problem set will count as the lowest, "dropped" grade for the semester.

## The Harvard College Honor Code

## Academic Integrity

Discussion and the exchange of ideas are essential to doing academic work. For assignments in this course, you are encouraged to consult with your classmates as you work on problem sets, lab worksheets, and projects.

**For problem sets and lab worksheets:** after discussions with peers, make sure that you can work through the problem yourself and ensure that any answers you submit for evaluation are the result of your own efforts. In addition, you must cite any books, articles, websites, lectures, etc. that have helped you with your work using appropriate citation practices. Similarly, you must list the names of students with whom you have collaborated on problem sets.

**For projects:** You are encouraged to consult with your classmates, the course staff, and others about your project. However, you should ensure that any written work you submit for evaluation is the result of your own research and writing. You must also adhere to standard citation practices and properly cite any books, articles, websites, lectures, etc. that have helped you with your work. Similarly, you must list the names of people whose feedback you have received on the project, and also include the nature of the feedback.

## Policy for Use of AI

This course allows students to explore the use of generative artificial intelligence (GAI) tools such as ChatGPT. Any such use must be appropriately acknowledged and cited. After exploring with GAI, make sure that you understand your answers and can work through the problem yourself. It is each student's responsibility to assess the validity and applicability of any GAI output that is submitted; you bear the final responsibility. Violations of this policy will be considered academic misconduct. We draw your attention to the fact that different classes at Harvard could implement different AI policies, and it is the student's responsibility to conform to expectations for each course.

**Special AI statement on problem sets:** The problem sets in this class are intended to help you learn and practice the material covered in class. They are also intended to help prepare you for the in-person exams, quizzes, and the final project. We urge you to be intentional in how you use the homeworks to maximize these goals. Ultimately you are responsible for learning the material. For example, you may find that not using GAI for homework problems can lead to deeper learning and retention, and honest assessment of your understanding, which may better prepare you for exams. After applying the general AI guidelines, make sure you fully understand all of your answers, and can complete all problems on your own. Note that all in-class exams and assignments will NOT allow collaboration nor use of GAI.
